▲ Safety, Skills & Resources

Help for Victim-Survivors.

Three things can help keep you safe: modern online skills, small changes to your daily habits, and knowing where to go for help.

§ A 21st-Century Skills to Spot and Stop Swatters

Shapiro (2023c) explains that staying safe online today takes a set of modern skills. Each one makes it harder for a swatter to find you or trick you.

  • Keep personal information private. Know what should stay private — home address, phone number, daily routine, workplace. Once something is posted online, it can be copied and shared forever.
  • Tell real from fake online. Learn to spot fake emails (phishing), fake phone calls (vishing), and fake texts (smishing) — the top tricks swatters use to steal info. Never click links or open files from people you don't know.
  • Use strong online security. Turn on two-step verification on every account, use long, unique passwords, and keep anti-virus software up to date.
  • Learn how online offenders work. When you understand the tricks swatters and doxers use, the tricks stop working on you — that is what this site is for.
  • Know when to ask for help. If someone is threatening you online, tell a trusted adult, friend, or the police. You don't have to handle it alone.

§ B Safety Advice Using Routine Activity Theory

Shapiro (2023c) uses Routine Activity Theory to explain how crime happens. The theory says a crime needs three things at the same time: a motivated offender, a suitable target, and an absence of protection. You cannot control whether swatters exist — but you can change how easy a target you are.

Reyns, Henson, and Fisher (2011) tested this on 974 college students and found four main risk factors. The tips below address each one.

Make Yourself Harder to Find

  • Limit what you post — your location, job, and anything that could be used to answer a security question.
  • Set social media, computers, and home Wi-Fi to "private."
  • Turn off location tracking and GPS when you're not using them.
  • For gamers: hide your IP address with a VPN and keep game settings private.

Block Strangers from Reaching You

  • Don't accept friend requests from strangers — Reyns et al. (2011) found this doubles your chance of being harassed.
  • Stay away from friends who act badly online — their behavior puts you at risk too.
  • Never share your IP address, real name, or home location on Discord, Skype, TeamSpeak, or Mumble.

Remove Your Info From Data Broker Sites

  • Ask sites like Spokeo, Intelius, and Acxiom to take down your info — most have an opt-out form.
  • Check your records with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion; fix any errors.
  • Ask your county to keep property records out of online searches, where state law allows.

If You Think You Are Being Targeted

  • Save everything — keep screenshots of threats and write down dates and platforms.
  • Tell your local police in advance. Some cities (like Seattle) let people at risk register their address so police know a fake call could come.
  • Ask the website hosting your leaked info to take it down.
  • Report suspicious emails to your internet provider.

§ C Websites and Strategies to Learn More and Get Help

Government Resources

  • Federal Trade Commission (FTC) — identity theft and personal information protection. ftc.gov
  • FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) — report online crimes. ic3.gov
  • CISA — guides on phishing and online scams. cisa.gov
  • StopBullying.gov — federal resource on online harassment. stopbullying.gov

Mental Health Support

  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — call or text 988, free and confidential, 24/7.
  • Crisis Text Line — text HOME to 741741.
  • SAMHSA National Helpline — 1-800-662-HELP (4357), free and confidential, 24/7.

Cyber Strategies to Keep Learning

  • Opt out of data broker sites using their removal forms.
  • Set up Google Alerts on your own name to catch new info posted about you.
  • Use a password manager to make and store strong passwords.
  • Review privacy settings on every social media account at least twice a year.
  • Take free online safety courses from your local library or staysafeonline.org.

▲ A final note

No list of tips removes all risk. These steps lower the chance and severity of being attacked by addressing the risk factors research has shown to matter most. The real fix is a federal law against swatting with penalties that match the harm. Prevention is the first step.

If you are in danger right now, call your local police. If you are struggling emotionally after an attack, you are not alone — help is available and swatting-related trauma can be treated.
