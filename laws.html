▲ The Legal Landscape

A Crime Without a Law.

No federal law specifically makes swatting a crime. Prosecutors have to mix and match older laws — and the results are very uneven sentences for nearly the same crime (Shapiro, 2023d).

§ 01 The Problem

Shapiro (2023d) outlines the legal gap:

  • There is no federal law that specifically makes swatting a crime.
  • Several bills have been introduced since 2015 (like H.R. 2031, the Anti-Swatting Act) — none have passed.
  • Prosecutors must put together charges from older laws, which leads to sentences that don't match the harm.

§ 02 Federal Laws Currently Used

Shapiro (2023b, p. 52; 2023d, pp. 101–105) lists the laws most often used against swatters.

18 U.S.C. § 875
Interstate Communications

Covers threats to kidnap or injure someone across state lines (875(c)); or threats to extort or damage property (875(b), (d)). Used when the fake 911 call reports violence.

Up to 5 / 20 years Fines up to $250,000
18 U.S.C. § 844(e)
Threats Involving Explosives

Covers threats or false information about bombs or explosives sent across state lines. Used for fake bomb threat calls.

Up to 10 years Fines up to $250,000
18 U.S.C. § 1038
False Information and Fake Reports

Covers sharing fake information about a federal crime that a reasonable person might believe. Used for the fake 911 call itself.

Up to 5 yrs / 20 if injury / life if death Fines up to $250,000
18 U.S.C. § 371
Conspiracy

Covers two or more people working together to commit a federal crime. Used often, since 71% of Shapiro's (2023b) swatting cases involved groups.

Up to 5 years Fines up to $250,000

Other charges that may be added (Shapiro, 2023b):

  • § 2261A — Cyberstalking (5 years to life).
  • § 119 — Sharing personal info about officials to threaten or scare them (up to 5 years).
  • §§ 1519 / 1512 — Destroying evidence and threatening witnesses.
  • §§ 1029 / 1030 — Computer and account fraud (when hacking is involved).

§ 03 What Prosecutors Must Prove

To convict someone of swatting under these federal laws, prosecutors have to prove two things beyond a reasonable doubt (Shapiro, 2023b; Shapiro, 2023d):

1. What the Person Did

That the offender made (or helped make) a fake 911 call across state lines that reported a violent emergency — such as a bomb threat, hostage situation, or active shooter — causing police to respond to a place where no real emergency was happening (Shapiro, 2023b; Shapiro, 2023d).

2. Why They Did It

That the offender acted on purpose. They had to know the report was false and intend to trigger the emergency response. Under § 844(e), the act must be "willful"; under § 1038, the offender must have "intended" to share false information. For conspiracy (§ 371), prosecutors also have to show that two or more people agreed to the plan and took at least one step to carry it out (Shapiro, 2023d).

§ 04 Why It's Hard to Prove

Shapiro (2023b; 2023d) explains the main barriers:

  • Evidence is hard to get. Swatters use fake caller ID, VoIP phones, and VPNs to hide. One officer spent 1,000+ hours over a year tracking a single minor swatter.
  • The crime crosses many places. An offender in one state or country; a call that looks local; a victim in yet another place; computer servers spread across the world.
  • Minors often don't show up in the numbers. Many swatters under 18 are never publicly charged.

§ 05 Are the Current Laws Enough?

Based on the evidence, the current laws do not appear to be enough. Without a single federal law specifically targeting swatting, sentences have been inconsistent and often far too light for the harm caused. Here are a few cases reviewed by Shapiro (2023d):

  • U.S. v. Islam: serial swatting, cyberstalking, and doxing of 50+ public figures → only 24 months in prison.
  • U.S. v. Hanshaw: swatting conspiracy that evacuated a hotel and closed streets → only 30 months.
  • Enzweiler (2015) argues that political swatting fits the legal definition of domestic terrorism under 18 U.S.C. § 2331(5): acts that are dangerous to life, meant to scare or control others, and done inside the U.S.
This article joins other legal experts in calling for Congress to create one clear law against swatting with the same definitions, penalties, and fines nationwide (Shapiro, 2023d).

▲ The bottom line

The current federal laws cover parts of swatting, but no single law covers the crime as a whole. Until Congress passes a specific swatting law with clear penalties and required payment to victims, prosecutors will keep piecing together charges — and offenders will keep getting sentences that don't match the harm they cause (Shapiro, 2023d).
