The Swatter.

Who makes these fake 911 calls, and why they say they do it.

§ 01 Who the Typical Swatter Is

Shapiro (2023b) looked at data on 48 arrested U.S. swatters. The offender profile is very consistent:

98% Male (47 of 48)
19 Most common age (range 15–43)
71% Part of the gaming community
  • Most are teenage or young adult white men.
  • The real number of swatters is probably much higher, because many — especially those under 18 — are never caught or charged.
  • In a group, members play different roles: the hacker, the person who spoofs the caller ID, the doxer (who finds the victim's info), the caller, and the live-streamer.

§ 02 Why They Do It

Shapiro (2023b) lists the main reasons swatters attack:

  • Personal anger or revenge — 64%. The biggest reason.
  • Bragging and attention. Swatters brag about how many people they have attacked.
  • Fun and entertainment. They enjoy watching the chaos happen.
  • Live-streaming. Some broadcast the raid to an online audience (Shapiro, 2023d).
  • Political intimidation. Enzweiler (2015) describes attacks on commentators like "Patterico" (John Frey) and CNN's Erik Erickson, used to scare them into silence.
By posting a victim's private information online, doxers can control, scare, embarrass, and put the target in real physical danger (Shapiro, 2023b).

§ 03 How They Trick the Victim

Shapiro (2023d, pp. 99–101) describes three steps most swatters follow.

Step 1 — Find Personal Information

  • Legally: Google searches, property records, social media, and data broker sites like Spokeo and Intelius.
  • Illegally: phishing emails, fake phone calls, fake text messages, and spying on chat apps like Discord and Skype.
  • Douglas (2016) describes three kinds of doxing: revealing someone's identity, sharing their address and private details, and exposing embarrassing information to hurt their reputation.

Step 2 — Fake the 911 Call

  • Caller ID spoofing makes the call appear to come from the victim's own phone number or a neighbor's.
  • 911 dispatchers trust the number on the screen, so police go to the victim's house — not the caller's.

Step 3 — Broadcast the Raid

  • In the gaming world, attacks are often live-streamed to maximize embarrassment.
  • Broadcasting turns one attack into entertainment for a crowd, making the damage worse.

§ 04 Why These Methods Work So Well

  • Anonymity removes the brakes. Shapiro (2023a) explains that when offenders hide their identity online, they lose the normal social limits that stop people from being cruel.
  • Personal info is cheap and everywhere. Data broker websites legally sell Americans' private info, and gamers often leak their IP addresses on chat apps (Shapiro, 2023b).
  • Police must take every call seriously. SWAT teams are trained to treat every 911 call as a real emergency. A confused victim's movements can be mistaken for reaching for a weapon (Shapiro, 2023b).

▲ The uneven risk

For the offender, swatting takes only a few minutes and a throwaway account. For the victim, it can cost their life. For the community, each incident costs about $10,000 to $25,000 (Shapiro, 2023b; Shapiro, 2023d). Until that gap closes, offenders have no real reason to stop.
